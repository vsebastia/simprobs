How to use this distribution calculator

This tool is designed to help you translate statistical concepts into concrete decisions. When you choose a calculation type like PDF/PMF, the calculator returns the curve height at a point (continuous distributions) or the exact probability of an integer value (discrete distributions). For the Normal distribution, the PDF output indicates density, not point probability; for Binomial and Poisson, the PMF result does represent point probability. If you switch to CDF, you get cumulative probability: for example, how likely it is to observe a value less than or equal to x, greater than or equal to x, or within an interval.

Quantile mode reverses the logic. Instead of entering a random variable value, you enter a probability between 0 and 1 and the tool returns the corresponding cutoff point. This approach is useful for percentiles, decision thresholds, and control limits. For example, if you need the value that leaves 95% of observations below it, select quantiles and enter 0.95.

In the chart, the red line or marker indicates the value used in the calculation. The shaded area represents the requested cumulative probability when using CDF: area to the left, area to the right, or area between two points. The larger the shaded area, the greater the probability of the defined event. If you want to connect theory and practice, check the Normal, Binomial, and Poisson sections below: there you will find when to use each distribution and how to map their parameters to the form fields.

Normal distribution

Plain-language explanation

The normal distribution models measurements that cluster around an average value and become less frequent as you move away from the center. Heights, exam scores, biological measurements, and many manufacturing variables often follow this shape when multiple small factors combine. Its famous bell curve is symmetric: values equally far above and below the mean are equally likely. In practice, this makes it one of the most important distributions in statistics because it supports confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, and quality monitoring. In this calculator, choose Normal when your variable is continuous and can reasonably take many values around a central tendency.

Formula

\( f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \)

Parameters

  • μ (mu): mean, or center of the distribution.
  • σ (sigma): standard deviation, which controls spread.

Real-world examples and usage

Suppose a factory fills bottles with a target of 500 ml. If machine variation is mostly random, fill volumes are often approximately normal. You can use PDF to inspect density near a target, CDF to compute the chance of being below a threshold, and quantiles to determine cut points for tolerance limits. In education, test scores are frequently summarized with normal-based percentiles. In finance and operations, normal approximations help estimate variability and service levels. To connect this explanation to the tool above, select Normal, enter your μ and σ, then compare how CDF and quantile outputs move as you change x or probability.

Binomial distribution

Plain-language explanation

The binomial distribution describes how many successes occur in a fixed number of independent yes/no trials. Each trial has the same probability of success, and the outcome count can only be an integer between 0 and n. This framework is ideal when you know the number of attempts in advance and want probabilities for counts, such as “exactly 7 successes” or “at most 3 failures.” Because it is discrete, the graph appears as bars or points rather than a smooth curve. In this calculator, choose Binomial whenever your data represent counts of success from repeated Bernoulli trials.

Formula

\( P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \)

Parameters

  • n: total number of trials.
  • p: probability of success in each trial.
  • k: number of observed successes.

Real-world examples and usage

Imagine an email campaign sent to 20 leads, with each lead having a 0.15 chance of conversion. The binomial model helps estimate the probability of seeing exactly 4 conversions, at least 5 conversions, or a conversion count within a planning range. In healthcare screening, it can model the number of positive tests in a batch with a known positivity rate. In quality control, it estimates defective units in sampled items. To use it interactively, select Binomial in the calculator, provide n and p, then switch between PMF for exact counts and CDF for cumulative ranges. Quantiles are especially useful for setting operational targets such as minimum expected successes at a chosen confidence level.

Poisson distribution

Plain-language explanation

The Poisson distribution models the number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time, area, or volume when events happen independently and at an approximately constant average rate. It is often used for rare-event counting: calls arriving per minute, defects per meter of material, or customer arrivals per hour. Unlike the binomial model, Poisson does not require a fixed number of trials; instead, it relies on an average rate. This makes it practical for processes where opportunities are continuous and counts accumulate naturally. In this calculator, choose Poisson when your variable is a nonnegative integer count tied to exposure units (time, space, transactions, etc.).

Formula

\( P(X=k)=\frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} \)

Parameters

  • λ (lambda): expected number of events in the interval.
  • k: observed event count.

Real-world examples and usage

A support desk might receive on average 6 tickets per hour. Poisson lets you estimate probabilities for exactly 10 tickets, no more than 4 tickets, or at least 8 tickets during the next hour. In logistics, it can model accidents per route; in public health, incident cases per day; in web analytics, clicks per minute for a low-traffic endpoint. In the calculator, set distribution to Poisson, enter λ, and evaluate PMF for exact counts or CDF for cumulative limits. Quantiles can help set staffing thresholds: for example, the 90th percentile gives a count that will not be exceeded in roughly 90% of intervals.

FAQ

What is a probability distribution?

A probability distribution is a mathematical description of how likely each possible outcome is for a random variable. Continuous distributions assign density over ranges, while discrete distributions assign probability mass to integer outcomes.

What is the difference between PDF and CDF?

PDF (or PMF in discrete cases) describes local probability behavior at a value, while CDF gives cumulative probability up to that value. CDF answers “how likely is X less than or equal to x?” and is often easier for decision thresholds.

How do I compute a quantile?

Select “Quantile” in the calculator and input a probability between 0 and 1. The returned value is the cutoff where the chosen proportion of outcomes lies below it.

When should I use a binomial distribution?

Use binomial when you have a fixed number of independent trials, each with only success/failure outcomes and a constant success probability.

What is the Poisson distribution used for?

Poisson is used for counting events in intervals when events occur independently at a stable average rate, such as arrivals, defects, or incidents over time.
